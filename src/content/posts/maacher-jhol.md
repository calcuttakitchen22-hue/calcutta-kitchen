## A Bengali Comfort Classic

Patla Macher Jhol is one of the most iconic everyday Bengali meals. Unlike rich restaurant-style fish curries, this light and comforting jhol relies on balance rather than heavy spices. With lightly fried fish, potatoes, green chillies, and a thin flavorful broth, it's the kind of meal that makes you reach for an extra serving of rice.


* Prep Time: 20 mins
* Cook Time: 30 mins
* Serves: 4

## Ingredients

- 6 pieces Rui fish (700-800 g)
- Salt to taste
- 1.5 tsp turmeric powder
- 2 medium potatoes, cut lengthwise
- 1 small tomato
- 3-4 green chillies, slit
- 1/2 tsp kalonji
- 1/2 tsp red chilli powder
- 1 tsp cumin powder
- 5 tbsp mustard oil
- 3.5-4 cups hot water

## Method

1. Marinate the fish with salt and turmeric and rest for 15-20 minutes
2. Heat mustard oil until smoking and lightly fry the fish until golden
3. Remove and keep aside
4. Fry the potatoes in the same oil with a little salt and turmeric until lightly golden
5. Add kalonji and green chillies
6. Add turmeric, red chilli powder, and cumin powder
7. Immediately add a splash of water and cook until the spices bubble smoothly
8. Add tomato and cook until softened
9. Pour in hot water and bring to a simmer
10. Add the potatoes and cook for 6-8 minutes
11. Gently add the fish pieces and simmer for 4-5 minutes
12. Avoid excessive stirring to prevent the fish from breaking
13. Cook until the jhol is thin, light, and slightly glossy
14. Serve hot with steamed rice

## Chef's Tips

- Do not over-marinate the fish as it can mask the fresh fish flavor
- Lightly fry the fish, do not make it crispy
- Add water immediately after the powdered spices to prevent burning
- This is a light jhol, not a kosha, so avoid aggressively frying the spices
- Judge the consistency visually rather than relying only on measurements
- Simmer gently after adding the fish to keep the pieces intact

## Frequently Asked Questions

- Why shouldn't the fish be fried until crispy? The fish continues cooking in the jhol and should remain soft and tender
- Can I skip the tomato? Yes, the tomato is optional and is used only to round off the flavor
- Why add water immediately after the spices? It prevents the spices from burning and keeps the jhol light
- How thin should the jhol be? It should mix beautifully with rice but still retain enough body and flavor
